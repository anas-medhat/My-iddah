My Divorce is the Biggest Blessing of My Life

Image Credit: Aneesa Sidat Written by: Maya Ali       I was desperate for my iddah […]

669

Share Tweet Pin it

Image Credit: Aneesa Sidat

Written by: Maya Ali

 
 
 

I was desperate for my iddah to start so I could begin learning the lessons of my marriage, my test.

 

I had a rough idea of what I wanted to work on first, but each day brought a new emotion and, in turn, an old memory I had tried so very hard to forget.  When everyone found out what I had been through during my marriage, I was told to just forget it all, like none of it had ever happened. At the time, I was hurt that I wasn’t allowed to share any memories, not even the happy ones–as rare as they were; No one wanted to hear them.

 

My initial iddah plan was to work on myself and not dwell on my ex-husband or the circumstances of my divorce. I decided to be selfish and think of what I needed, as I failed to do this during the two and a half years I was married.

 

I began to seek forgiveness and get closer Allah (SWT) during my iddah, and alhamdulillah before my iddah ended I managed to forgive myself, him, his family, and the circumstances in which my marriage ended.

 

More importantly, I found myself.  I learned that true peace and contentment comes from emotional and psychological independence, not financial independence as I had previously thought.  This was all only possible due to praying on time, praying the night prayer, hours of dhikr, learning Allah’s (SWT) names and understanding them, listening to many stories of the women of Islam, but more importantly reading and understanding the Qur’an.

 

I didn’t want my iddah to end. I enjoyed putting my prayers first, putting myself first and mostly I enjoyed the solitude. I’d been alone many times before but this kind of loneliness was blessed with the presence of Allah (SWT),  His infinite mercy, and His company.  I realised He didn’t mind how many times I shared a story. He didn’t frown or give me awkward looks when I mentioned my ex’s name. He didn’t tell me to forget everything. He allowed me to look for the lessons and learn from them. He allowed me to grieve without judging me for talking about someone who did nothing but hurt me. He allowed me time to heal.

 

It sounds so simple and had someone told me how peaceful it is to just trust Allah (SWT), I probably wouldn’t have believed them until I was given a lesson that would make me reflect and eventually learn how to find inner peace.

 

Whenever people hear that I’ve been divorced they tend to say ‘I’m so sorry to hear that’. I reply “My divorce is the biggest blessing of my life.”

 

I wrote the following the day before my iddah was due to end and alhamdulillah, 6 months since my iddah ended,  I still feel at peace.

 

19.02.16
Dear End of Chapter,

 

As scary as coming out of iddah sounded,  as anxious as facing the world seemed, as unsettling as the future may be, right now, on the eve of my 3-month social sabbatical, I feel at peace. Peace, relief, and gratitude.

 

Now when I see his face, it makes me smile and not cringe. I Smile because of anyone I could have experienced this with,  I’m glad it was him.  I’m sad we couldn’t be better for each other for the sake of Allah (SWT) but grateful we’re both on the right path now, grateful our marriage taught us what we needed to know in order to grow, learn, and be better versions of ourselves for The One who created us.

 

The fact that it ended before we completely destroyed what was left of each other is such a huge blessing. I must admit I worried about where his head was at and what he was doing and how he was coping. But for some strange reason, I feel reassured that he’s OK. This lesson has taught him what he needed in order to put into action all those intentions he used to say he had. I hope.

I know I won’t always feel this grateful,  but right now, in this moment, I’m very  grateful.

 

I have no desire to see him, talk to him or even know about what he is doing.  I ask God to tell him I have forgiven him and everything he let happen to me and hope, inshaAllah, one day he can forgive me, too: forgive me for not staying passively patient; forgive me for not being able to continue to allow him and his family to devalue my worth; and forgive me for allowing him and his family to determine my worth in the first place.

 

I pray Allah (swt) forgives me for trying to gain acceptance from people by making them happy instead of gaining reward from Him first, in fact, solely for His sake.

 

Less than 24 hours to go, inshaAllah.

 
 
 

Maya Ali is a 34-year-old educator living in the UK. She enjoys reading and cooking. She has a passion for learning and loves spending time with family.
